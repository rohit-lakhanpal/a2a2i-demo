# Sample Transcript — Grant Fit Check Agent

> This is a real transcript from a test run of the **Grant Fit Check** agent — the custom agent built in Copilot Agent Builder during Stage 2 using instructions from the Instruction Generator. This transcript shows the agent assessing a live Victorian Government grant against the fictitious org's SharePoint documents.

---

## Setup

The Grant Fit Check agent was configured in Copilot Agent Builder with:
- **Instructions** generated by the Instruction Generator Agent (with the [Knowledge section overridden](instruction-generator.md#%EF%B8%8F-gotcha-knowledge-source-override) for the demo)
- **SharePoint** connected to the "Southern Community Collective Inc" team site containing 5 org profile documents
- **Web browsing** enabled for reading live grant pages
- **Only use specified sources** toggled ON

## Test 2 — Full Grant Assessment

> 💬 **You**
>
> We're considering applying for this grant: https://www.vic.gov.au/community-food-relief-program-local-grants
>
> Please assess whether Southern Community Collective is a good fit. Our org documents are in SharePoint. The grant guidelines DOCX has also been uploaded there.

> 🤖 **Grant Fit Check**
>
> ## Decision Block (early go/no-go)
>
> **Fit score: *Medium* (project-dependent)**
>
> **Why it fits:**
> - The grant targets **local food relief / food security activities in Victoria** and requires **direct food relief** as part of every project. Your core programs already do this (meals + hampers).
> - **Southern Community Collective Inc.** is positioned like the "community and volunteer-led organisations" the program calls out, and operates locally in Melbourne's Bayside area.
>
> **Key blocker / risk:**
> - The program explicitly excludes **core operational expenses such as rent and current wages**. Your stated highest-priority funding need is **Kitchen Coordinator salary** and **facility lease** — which would likely be considered core ops and therefore ineligible.
>
> **Best next steps (actionable):**
> - Pursue this grant **only if you can frame a project** around eligible categories (e.g., equipment, infrastructure, additional delivery capacity, volunteer training/recruitment, partnership building) **without** using funds for existing lease/current wages.
> - Note: the program page currently shows **Status: Closed** (so treat this as fit assessment for a future round or if it reopens).
>
> **One question (so I can give a sharper go/no-go):**
> What's the project you'd want to submit under this grant — **equipment/infrastructure**, **expanding pantry/meals**, **training/volunteer uplift**, or something else?
>
> ---
>
> ## Detailed Breakdown
>
> ### 1) Grant snapshot (from the public program page)
>
> - **Program:** Community Food Relief Program – Local Grants
> - **Funding range:** $15,000–$100,000
> - **Status:** Closed
> - **Must-have feature of funded projects:** "All projects must involve an element of providing direct food relief to the community."
>
> **Examples of what can be funded (eligible activity types):**
> - Equipment/appliances (buy/hire/install)
> - Infrastructure/facility improvements (kitchens, food storage, etc.)
> - Program supplies incl. nutritious/culturally appropriate food + packaging materials
> - Partnership/network building + strategic planning to strengthen food relief activities
> - Training and development to build staff/volunteer skills, knowledge and safety
> - Program delivery costs including extra staff, volunteer recruitment, community education/awareness/promotional activities
> - Up to 10% can be allocated to project management/admin that directly supports the project
>
> **What the page says cannot be funded (high relevance):**
> - Core operational expenses such as rent, insurance, current wages, overheads and consumables
> - Activities started before approval (retrospective) and activities extending beyond 30 June 2027
>
> ### 2) Southern Community Collective Inc. — what I found in SharePoint docs
>
> **Core purpose & geography:**
> - Vision/mission emphasises food access and practical support in Melbourne's Bayside region (Brighton/Hampton/Sandringham).
>
> **Direct food relief delivery (strong alignment):**
> - **Community Kitchen:** weekly shared meals, ~60–80 people/session; uses rescued/donated food.
> - **Pantry Share:** fortnightly hampers ~120 households/cycle; includes culturally appropriate options; demand exceeds supply.
> - Demonstrated growth in demand (meals +14%, hampers +11% year-on-year in FY24–25).
>
> **Partnerships/capability (supports competitive application):**
> - Partnerships noted with OzHarvest, Foodbank Victoria, Bayside Community Health, City of Bayside, etc.
> - Governance & compliance indicators include insurance, audit, food safety registration and "no overdue reports."
>
> **Your stated "highest priority" funding need (potential mismatch):**
> - Strategic plan flags "non-negotiable" need to cover Kitchen Coordinator salary ($68K p.a.) and facility lease ($24K p.a.) to sustain services.
> - Annual report repeats that rising lease and staff costs are the key challenge.
>
> ### 3) Alignment check (criteria-by-criteria)
>
> **A. Direct food relief requirement — ✅ Strong match**
> - Grant requires direct food relief element. SCC delivers meals + hampers as core services.
>
> **B. Eligible activity types — ✅ Many good angles**
> SCC has credible options that map cleanly onto eligible categories:
> - Cold storage / kitchen upgrades to increase safe capacity and reduce waitlists (infrastructure/equipment)
> - Packaging + culturally appropriate food inputs for Pantry Share (program supplies)
> - Volunteer recruitment/training/safety uplift to meet growing demand (training + recruitment)
> - "Extra staff" for expansion (e.g., additional session capacity) rather than "current wages"
>
> **C. Biggest eligibility/fit risk — ⚠️ Potential blocker depending on budget framing**
> - Grant excludes rent and current wages as core ops. SCC's stated top priority is exactly those categories (lease + coordinator salary).
> - ➡️ You likely need to **pivot** the request to an expansion/capacity project (eligible) rather than underwriting baseline operations (ineligible).
>
> **D. Timing — ⚠️ Not currently open**
> - Page shows Status: Closed. Fit is still worth assessing, but treat this as "good to prepare for next opening/reopening" rather than immediate submission.
>
> ### 4) Recommended "best-fit" project framings (based strictly on what's eligible)
>
> 1. **Capacity uplift for Pantry Share (reduce waitlist)** — storage equipment, refrigeration, shelving; packaging; culturally appropriate food inputs; volunteer training and recruitment; delivery/logistics costs.
> 2. **Community Kitchen infrastructure + equipment refresh** — appliances, safe food handling upgrades, storage improvements, training/safety — linked to serving more meals or improving nutritional/cultural appropriateness.
> 3. **Partnership/network strengthening to increase supply** — activities to build partnerships and strategic planning that strengthen food relief (still anchored by direct food relief delivery).
>
> **Avoid (or be very careful):**
> - Any budget lines that look like existing lease or existing staff base salary (described as "current wages").
>
> ### 5) Missing / unclear info I couldn't confirm from documents
>
> - I haven't located your uploaded grant guidelines DOCX in SharePoint yet — my file search returned the internal summary docs (Strategic Plan Summary.md, Capability Statement.md, Annual Report Summary.md) but not the guidelines DOCX you mentioned.
> - I *did* use the public program page for the criteria above.
>
> ---
>
> If you answer my one question (which project concept you want to submit), I'll convert this into a crisp **go/no-go** plus a **"most defensible" draft project scope** that stays safely inside eligible cost categories.

## How Good Is This Answer?

Cross-referencing against the [Cheat Sheet](../cheat-sheet.md), the agent's response reveals both its strengths and a deliberate gap:

| Designed Disqualifier | Caught? | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Round is closed** | ✅ Yes | Agent flagged "Status: Closed" from the grant web page |
| **2. Excluded use of funds** | ✅ Yes | Agent correctly identified that salary + lease = core ops, which the grant explicitly excludes |
| **3. Geographic priority mismatch** | ❌ No | Agent noted Bayside as the org's location but **did not flag** that Brighton/Bayside is affluent and the grant prioritises regional/rural Victoria and pockets of significant disadvantage (25% weighting criterion) |

**Why this matters for the demo:**

The agent caught **2 out of 3** disqualifiers — which is genuinely useful but not perfect. This is exactly the setup you need for the Trust & Safety conversation in Stage 3:

- It demonstrates real value (the core ops exclusion is buried in guidelines and easy to miss)
- It shows AI isn't infallible (the geographic mismatch was there to find)
- It validates the human-in-the-loop principle — the grants officer still needs to review the output

**Why this is also perfect for iterating on the instructions:**

The geographic miss isn't a flaw in the technology — it's a gap in the instructions. The agent wasn't told to weight geographic priority criteria or compare the org's location against disadvantage indexes. This is a natural prompt to say to the audience:

> *"Now that we've seen what it caught and what it missed — what would you change in the instructions to close that gap? Maybe add a rule: 'Always check if the grant has geographic priority criteria and compare against the org's LGA.' That's how you iterate. Run the agent, see what it misses, improve the instructions, run it again."*

This turns the miss from a failure into a teaching moment about **iterative instruction design** — the same loop participants will use with their own agents after the workshop.

> **Recovery phrase if this comes up during the demo:**
> *"It caught two out of three. In a real workflow, the Trust & Safety layer helps close that gap."*
> — from the [Cheat Sheet](../cheat-sheet.md)

Also note the agent scored it as **Medium** rather than the expected **NO-GO**. This is because without the full guidelines DOCX in SharePoint, it's working from the public web page which doesn't surface the geographic weighting criteria as clearly. The agent is being appropriately cautious (strict flagging mode) rather than overcommitting to a verdict with incomplete data.

## What the Agent Did Well

This response demonstrates exactly why a custom agent is valuable — and why this scenario works as a demo:

**1. It read a live government website and extracted structured criteria.**
The agent browsed the [Victorian Government grant page](https://www.vic.gov.au/community-food-relief-program-local-grants), parsed the eligible and ineligible activity types, funding range, and status — then presented them in a clean, scannable format. This isn't summarisation; it's structured extraction with intent.

**2. It cross-referenced the grant against internal documents — automatically.**
The agent pulled the org's strategic plan, capability statement, and annual report from SharePoint, then compared the grant's requirements against what the org actually does, what it needs funding for, and what partnerships it has. A human doing this manually would need to have both the grant page and five org documents open side by side.

**3. It caught a buried disqualifier that a human would likely miss.**
The core ops exclusion — "Funds cannot be used for core operational expenses such as rent, insurance, current wages, overheads" — is easy to skim past. But the agent connected it to the org's strategic plan, which flags Kitchen Coordinator salary ($68K) and facility lease ($24K) as the top funding priority. That cross-reference is the whole point of the agent: it reads the fine print *and* knows your org well enough to flag the conflict.

**4. It didn't just say no — it said what to do instead.**
Rather than stopping at "this is a blocker," the agent suggested three concrete project framings that would stay within eligible cost categories. It even flagged which budget lines to avoid. This turns a rejection into a roadmap.

**5. It was transparent about what it didn't know.**
The agent explicitly noted that it couldn't find the guidelines DOCX in SharePoint and used the public web page instead. It flagged this as a gap rather than pretending it had complete information. This is the "strict flagging" behaviour we configured in Stage 2.

**6. It asked a follow-up question to sharpen the verdict.**
Instead of forcing a premature go/no-go, the agent asked which project concept the user would submit — acknowledging that the fit depends on how the application is framed, not just whether the org is eligible. This shows genuine decision-support behaviour, not just checkbox checking.

**What it missed (by design):**
The geographic priority mismatch — Brighton/Bayside is an affluent area, and the grant prioritises "regional and rural Victoria, drought-affected communities, pockets of significant disadvantage in Melbourne" with 25% weighting. This criterion is buried in the downloadable guidelines (which the agent couldn't find in SharePoint) and only partially referenced on the public web page. The miss is deliberate: it sets up the Stage 3 conversation about why human-in-the-loop, guardrails, and Trust & Safety reviews are essential — not optional.

## Why This Is a Good Demo

This scenario was chosen because it naturally showcases what Copilot Agent Builder is good at — and why a custom agent is the right tool (not just base Copilot).

### It uses a real grant from a real government website

The agent browses a live Victorian Government grant page, extracts structured criteria, and cross-references them against internal documents in SharePoint. This isn't a toy example — it's a real grant with real eligibility rules, and the audience can see the agent reading it in real time.

### It proves the case for "custom" over "basic Copilot"

Could you ask regular Copilot to summarise this grant? Sure. But this agent does something different:
- It **reads the fine print** and compares every criterion against the org's own profile
- It **catches a buried disqualifier** (core ops exclusion vs. the org's top funding priority) that a human would likely miss
- It knows the right answer is sometimes **"talk to Finance"** rather than guessing
- It **refuses to overcommit** when data is incomplete — flagging gaps instead of fabricating confidence

That's the difference between a summariser and a decision-support agent.

### The M365 connectors feel natural, not forced

| Connector | What it does in the demo | Why it's organic |
|-----------|-------------------------|-----------------|
| **SharePoint** | Reads the org's profile, strategic plan, capability statement, past grants, annual report | Every nonprofit keeps internal docs somewhere — SharePoint is the natural M365 home |
| **Public web** | Reads the live grant page, extracts eligibility criteria, checks status | Grant opportunities live on government websites — web browsing is the obvious connector |
| **Org chart** *(available but off for demo)* | Could route "who to talk to" recommendations | Shows the art of the possible without over-engineering the demo |

### It deliberately fails in a useful way

The agent catches 2 out of 3 designed disqualifiers but misses the geographic priority mismatch. This is the most powerful moment in the demo because it:
- Shows the agent is **genuinely useful** (it caught things a human would miss)
- Proves the agent **isn't infallible** (it missed something that was there to find)
- **Sets up Stage 3** naturally — the Trust & Safety conversation about guardrails, human-in-the-loop, and responsible deployment isn't theoretical; it has a concrete example

### It resonates emotionally

The hook — *"Spent two weeks writing an application, rejected because of a criterion buried on page 12"* — lands with every nonprofit and university in the room. They've all lived it. The demo doesn't just show technology; it shows technology solving a problem the audience has personally felt.

### It's the right level of complexity

Not trivial (a simple summariser wouldn't work), not over-engineered (no complex integrations or custom code). The agent does structured extraction, multi-document cross-referencing, criteria-by-criteria assessment, and actionable recommendations — all within what Copilot Agent Builder can deliver out of the box.
