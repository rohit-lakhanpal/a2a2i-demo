# Sample Transcript — Research Agent

> This is a real transcript from a test run of the Research Agent. Use it as a reference for what the conversation looks like in practice. The facilitator role-plays as a staff member at Southern Community Collective.

---

## Opening

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> Welcome — I'll be your facilitator in this Research & Definition space.
>
> To begin, share whatever is clearest for you. You can:
> - describe a situation that feels stuck, risky, or frustrating
> - name a group of people you work with or serve
>
> Once you share something, I'll guide you with focused questions, one at a time, until we land on a clear problem and primary audience.

---

## Beat 1 — Start Broad

> 💬 **You**
>
> We're a small community nonprofit in Melbourne and we spend too much time chasing grants we don't win. We find opportunities, get excited, start writing, and then discover halfway through — or after we submit — that we were never a good fit. It's exhausting and it's burning out our grants officer.

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> Thank you for sharing that — it's a very clear pain point.
>
> Let's zoom in on a concrete moment when this feels most frustrating for your team.
>
> Can you describe a recent example — what was happening when you realized "we're not a good fit"?
> (For instance: was it during the application writing, after submission, or when reviewing eligibility?)

---

## Beat 2 — The Painful Moment

> 💬 **You**
>
> Last quarter we spent two weeks writing an application for a state government food relief grant. Detailed project plan, budget, partner letters — the lot. Got rejected. Turns out there was a geographic priority criterion buried in the downloadable guidelines document that we didn't match. Two weeks of Sarah's time, gone. And Sarah's our CEO — she shouldn't be doing this at all but we can't afford a dedicated grants person.

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> That paints a vivid picture — thank you.
>
> So in that moment, the biggest pain was discovering a hidden eligibility detail after investing significant time and leadership effort.
>
> Next question: Who is most affected when this happens? Is it primarily Sarah (as CEO), the whole leadership team, or someone else who feels the brunt of this wasted effort?

---

## Beat 3 — Identify the Persona

> 💬 **You**
>
> Honestly, it's whoever has time. Usually Sarah, sometimes Tom our Program Manager. But the person who *should* be doing it — or at least the first-pass screening — is someone in a grants officer role. We're trying to get a part-time grants coordinator but haven't hired yet. In the meantime, anyone who spots an opportunity just starts chasing it.

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> Got it — so the intended owner is a grants officer (or coordinator), but right now the burden falls on senior staff like Sarah and Tom, which amplifies the cost of a bad fit.
>
> Here's a draft Primary Persona based on what you've shared:
>
> **Primary Persona:**
> A part-time Grants Coordinator (or equivalent) responsible for identifying, screening, and preparing grant applications for your nonprofit. They need to quickly assess which opportunities are worth pursuing and which are not, so they can focus limited time on the best-fit grants.
>
> Does that sound right for the role you want to design around? Or would you tweak it?

> 💬 **You**
>
> Yeah, that's right — the grants officer, or whoever is doing that role on any given week. They need to quickly figure out: is this grant worth our time? Before anyone spends a day on it.

---

## Beat 4 — Confirm the Problem

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> Perfect — that gives us a clear anchor.
>
> Here's a draft Problem Statement for validation:
>
> **Problem Statement:**
> Our nonprofit wastes significant time and leadership energy on grant applications that are a poor fit. The root issue is that whoever is acting as grants officer struggles to quickly and confidently assess eligibility and alignment before investing effort, leading to burnout and missed opportunities to focus on the right grants.
>
> Does this capture the core problem from your perspective? Or would you adjust any part of it?

> 💬 **You**
>
> The core problem is that we can't tell the difference between "this looks relevant" and "this is actually a fit for us" without reading 30 pages of guidelines and cross-referencing against our own org details. And we don't have the time or expertise to do that systematically. So we either skip opportunities or waste time on wrong ones.

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> That's an important nuance — it's not just wasted time, it's the lack of a fast, systematic way to judge fit without deep reading and expertise.
>
> Here's a refined Problem Statement:
>
> **Problem Statement:**
> Our nonprofit lacks a quick, reliable way to determine whether a grant is truly a good fit without reading lengthy guidelines and manually cross-checking against our own eligibility and priorities. As a result, we either skip opportunities or waste significant time on applications that fail, draining limited staff capacity and leadership energy.
>
> Does this version feel accurate?

> 💬 **You**
>
> Yeah

---

## Beat 5 — Jobs to Be Done

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> Great — we have a validated problem and persona.
>
> Next, let's define the Job To Be Done (JTBD) in a simple format:
>
> **Draft JTBD:**
> When I find a new grant opportunity, I want to quickly assess whether it's a strong fit for our organization, so I can decide early whether to pursue it and avoid wasting time on low-probability applications.
>
> Does that capture the essence of what this person needs? Or would you tweak it?

> 💬 **You**
>
> What the grants officer really needs to do is: paste a grant opportunity, have it checked against our org profile, eligibility, strategic fit, and get a quick go/no-go with reasons. And if it's a no-go, tell us why and what we'd need to change. And if it's a go, tell us who in the org to talk to first — like who owns the budget, who has the program data, who signs off.

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> That's super helpful context — it reinforces the core motivation and desired outcome.
>
> Here's an updated JTBD that reflects that intent without jumping into solution mode:
>
> **JTBD:**
> When I come across a potential grant, I want to know quickly whether it's a good fit for our organization and what key steps or people are involved if we proceed, so I can make an informed go/no-go decision without wasting time or missing critical inputs.
>
> Does that feel right? Or would you adjust any part of it?

> 💬 **You**
>
> Looks good

---

## Beat 6 — Use Case Selection

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> Excellent — we now have:
>
> - **Primary Persona:** Grants Coordinator (or whoever fills that role)
> - **Problem Statement:** Validated
> - **JTBD:** Validated
>
> The next step is to brainstorm specific ways an AI copilot could help — focusing on guidance, clarity, and decision support. These are hypotheses, not final solutions.
>
> **1. Quick Fit Check** — Summarizes a grant opportunity and highlights key eligibility criteria, then compares them to your org's profile to flag major fit issues.
>
> **2. Go/No-Go Decision Brief** — Produces a short decision brief with reasons, plus what would need to change for a better fit.
>
> **3. Internal Prep Checklist** — If "Go," lists the internal steps and who to involve.
>
> **4. Grant Landscape Snapshot** — Tracks and summarizes all current opportunities under consideration.
>
> **5. Lessons Learned Analyzer** — After a rejection, reviews feedback and identifies patterns.
>
> Which one feels most urgent and impactful for you right now?

> 💬 **You**
>
> Maybe number 1. My initial idea was a Grant Opportunity Assessment Assistant. That's exactly it. If we can get the screening right, everything downstream gets easier. No point drafting faster if we're drafting for the wrong grants.

---

## Beat 7 — Full Summary + Handoff

> 🤖 **Agent**
>
> Perfect — that's a strong and focused choice.
>
> Here's your final structured summary for handoff:
>
> **Context:**
> A small community nonprofit in Melbourne struggles with wasted time and leadership energy on grant applications that turn out to be a poor fit.
>
> **Problem Statement (validated):**
> Our nonprofit lacks a quick, reliable way to determine whether a grant is truly a good fit without reading lengthy guidelines and manually cross-checking against our own eligibility and priorities. As a result, we either skip opportunities or waste significant time on applications that fail, draining limited staff capacity and leadership energy.
>
> **Primary Persona (validated):**
> A part-time Grants Coordinator (or whoever fills that role) responsible for identifying, screening, and preparing grant applications.
>
> **Primary JTBD (validated):**
> When I come across a potential grant, I want to know quickly whether it's a good fit for our organization and what key steps or people are involved if we proceed, so I can make an informed go/no-go decision without wasting time or missing critical inputs.
>
> **Prioritised Use Case — Grant Opportunity Assessment Assistant:**
> An AI copilot that reviews a grant opportunity, extracts key eligibility and priority criteria, and compares them to the organization's profile to flag major fit issues.
>
> **Key Constraints:**
> Must work with unstructured grant guidelines (PDFs, web pages). Needs a simple way to reference the org's profile. Should provide clear reasoning for its assessment to build trust.
